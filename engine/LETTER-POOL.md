# Letter Pool Generator

How `engine/board.ts` builds a board, why it's built this way, and how to
change it without breaking things.

## Overview at a glance

A board is generated by drawing letters from **three pools** (vowels,
backbone consonants, others), with **exact per-board counts** for each
pool, then shuffling all cells. Per-board pool counts and per-pool letter
weights are tuned **per board size** (4×4, 5×5, 6×6) — each size has its
own config.

The model replaces the predecessor dice-based generator. It ties or beats
that baseline on every metric the calibration harness measures (common-
word count, length-6+ count, coverage, concentration) without using
reject sampling.

Production config lives in `engine/board.ts` as `SIZE_CONFIG`. That const
is the source of truth. The interactive `/calibrate` test bench that
informed the original tuning has been removed from the codebase now that
H8 is settled — all future tuning flows through the CLI scripts described
below.

## What's in the model

**Three pools per size:**

| Pool | Letters | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Vowel** | A, E, I, O, U | The cells that make words connectable. Pinning the count is the single biggest variance reduction vs IID. |
| **Backbone** | T, S, R, N, L, D | The consonants that anchor most English morphology (`-ER`, `-ED`, `-ING`, plurals, dental stops). |
| **Other** | B C F G H J K M P Qu V W X Y Z | Everything else. Includes the rare letters (J/X/Z), Y (ambiguous vowel/consonant), and the Qu tile. |

**Per-size config (`SIZE_CONFIG` in `engine/board.ts`):**

| | 4×4 | 5×5 | 6×6 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **V (exact vowel count)** | 5 | 10 | 13 |
| **B (exact backbone count)** | 7 | 9 | 14 |
| **O (= size² − V − B)** | 4 | 6 | 9 |

Within each pool, letters are sampled IID with per-size weights (also in
`SIZE_CONFIG`). Each size's weights are derived from that size's
historical dice frequencies, with bare Q faces merged into Qu.

**Generation algorithm** (see `generateBoardCore` in `engine/board.ts`):

1. Build three CDFs from the size's pool weights.
2. Draw V letters from the vowel CDF (using the provided RNG).
3. Draw B letters from the backbone CDF.
4. Draw (size² − V − B) letters from the other CDF.
5. Fisher-Yates shuffle all cells using the same RNG.
6. Pack into a 2D grid.

**Determinism:** `generateSeededBoard(size, seed)` calls
`generateBoardCore` with `mulberry32(seed)`. Same seed + same config →
same board, every device, every time. `generateBoard()` (no seed) uses
`Math.random` for freeplay.

The RNG flows through *every* random choice (letter sampling AND shuffle),
so determinism is end-to-end and matches what the dice setup guaranteed.

## What changes (and what doesn't) if you tweak SIZE_CONFIG

- Letter weights and quotas are read at module load — change them and
  every newly-generated board reflects the new config immediately.
- Historical daily seeds still map to *some* board, but the resulting
  letters change when the config changes. Stored `daily_results.board`
  rows preserve the played grid, so historical display and scoring are
  unaffected.
- The `generateBoard` / `generateSeededBoard` public signatures don't
  change; downstream callers (server's daily route, GameController,
  client's seed-derived flows) don't need updates.

## How the config was reached

The current values came out of a calibration process documented in
`scripts/calibrate-letters.ts` and `scripts/tune-quotas.ts`. Short
chronological log of the major decisions:

### 1. Why three pools at all (vs IID with a single weight table)?

We started with single-pool IID variants (Norvig text frequencies,
Scrabble tile frequencies, dice-derived frequencies). They consistently
**lost to the dice on 5×5/6×6 boards by 2–6 common words per board**
across every weight setting we tried.

Root cause: same-mean IID has wider per-board variance than the dice's
per-position constraints. On a right-skewed metric (common-word count is
bounded ≥0), wider variance pulls the median below the mean — even when
mean letter rates match exactly.

To recover the dice's structural advantage without bringing the dice
metaphor back, we needed to **pin per-board variance on the letter
categories that matter most for word formation**. Vowels are obviously
critical (no vowels → no playable words). Backbone consonants (T S R N L
D) cover most English morphology. Pinning exact counts on both classes
gives same-mean IID a structural floor.

### 2. Why backbone = {T S R N L D} specifically?

Empirically, these six letters appear in the highest proportion of
common English word *endings* and *frames*:
- Plurals: `-S`
- Past tense: `-ED` (D, plus a vowel)
- Present participle: `-ING` (N)
- Agent/comparative: `-ER` (R)
- Adverb: `-LY` (L)
- Plus T as the most common consonant in English

The exact set was less important than "a group of ~6 high-yield consonants
with an exact count." Could have been {T S R N L M} or similar with
comparable results. The current set was chosen by inspection plus
calibration validation.

### 3. Why these specific V/B/O numbers?

Quotas were grid-searched (`scripts/tune-quotas.ts`) per size, with a
constraint: don't regress on any metric vs the dice. Sweep ranged ±2
around the dice's mean vowel + backbone counts. The winning quotas were:

- **4×4**: V=5 (vs dice mean 4.7), B=7, O=4. B was bumped from 6→7 in
  tuning — one more backbone consonant added value.
- **5×5**: V=10 (vs dice mean 9.5), B=9, O=6. Local optimum — every other
  candidate regressed on at least one metric.
- **6×6**: V=13 (vs dice mean 14.0), B=14, O=9. Slight under-vowel by 1
  paid for an extra backbone.

The pattern: in two of three sizes, the tuner traded one vowel/other for
one more backbone consonant. The dice were *almost* optimally tuned by
hand decades ago.

### 4. Why per-size weights instead of global?

Each size's dice had different letter mixes. The 4×4 dice were balanced;
5×5 and 6×6 dice leaned vowel- and T-heavy. Production preserves the
per-size character of those mixes — the 4×4 config has roughly equal A/E
weight while 6×6's vowel pool is E-dominant (E:28 vs A:16). The
calibration validated per-size variants; using global weights would lose
that size-specific tuning.

(The original interactive test bench at `/calibrate` used global weights
with per-size quotas as a tuning simplification. It was removed after
H8 landed; the CLI tools below cover the same ground for future tuning.)

### 5. Why exact counts instead of probabilistic floors?

Earlier attempts used a "floor V" (draw at least V vowels, maybe more
from a shared pool). That left vowels probabilistic on top of the floor,
which kept the IID variance problem. Pinning to *exact* counts eliminates
the variance entirely on the pinned dimensions — the median jumps to
match the mean. This was the single biggest win in the calibration
journey, taking H8 from "loses by 1–3 words on bigger boards" to "ties or
beats baseline everywhere."

### 6. Why merge bare Q faces into Qu?

The original dice had one die with a bare Q and one die with a Qu tile.
Bare Q is effectively a dead letter (no English words have a non-Qu Q
without surrounding context). Merging Q faces into Qu doubles the Qu
rate, which:
- Matches the standard word-game convention of always treating Q as part of Qu
- Removes a dead-cell failure mode
- Keeps the Qu rate close to the dice's effective rate (still ~17%/17%/42%
  per-board across sizes)

### 7. Why three pools and not four or two?

Two pools (vowels + everything else) didn't recover the dice's
backbone-consonant structure. We tried this — it was "option F" in the
calibration. F lost to the dice by 1–3 words on bigger boards.

Four pools (e.g., vowels / backbones / mid-frequency / rare) was tested
informally but didn't add value over three: the "other" pool is already
small enough on 4×4 (4 cells) and 6×6 (9 cells) that further stratifying
it doesn't change much. Three was the minimum that beat baseline.

## Calibration metrics

The calibration harness scores candidate models on these axes:

1. **Common-word count** — words a board produces that appear in MIT-10k.
   Primary attainability metric, though now supplemented by SUBTLEX tiers.

2. **Length-6+ count** — number of 6+ letter words. Ceiling/satisfaction
   metric; long words score most under the Fibonacci scoring.

3. **Length distribution** — per-board histogram of word lengths.
   Sanity-check that boards aren't dominated by 3-letter words.

4. **Coverage** — fraction of common words at each length seen at least
   once across N boards. Variety-breadth: are we surfacing many distinct
   words, or recycling the same set?

5. **Concentration** — % of common-word appearances coming from the
   top-20 most-repeated words. Variety-evenness: lower is flatter.

6. **Suffix counts** — per-board mean of `-ed / -er / -ing / -tion / -ly /
   -y / -ers / -ier / -est` endings, longest-match. Morphological density.

7. **SUBTLEX tier counts** *(opt-in)* — per-board count of 4+ letter
   words at each Zipf tier (easy ≥4, medium 3–4, hard 2–3, impossible
   <2). Gradient of attainability beyond binary MIT-10k membership.
   Useful for diagnosing "boards feel too easy / too rare" without
   pre-committing to a binary cutoff.

   The SUBTLEX data file is **not committed to the repo** — the original
   dataset (Brysbaert & New, 2009) is "freely available for research"
   but the commercial-use status is fuzzy enough that we don't
   redistribute it. The metric runs **only when** `scripts/data/subtlex.tsv`
   is present locally. Calibrations without SUBTLEX still produce the
   other six metrics normally.

   To enable the tier metric: download the SUBTLEX-US zip from Ghent,
   unzip into `scripts/data/_tmp/`, then `npx tsx scripts/preprocess-subtlex.ts`.
   Full instructions in `scripts/data/README.md`.

All metrics evaluated at N=1000 boards minimum. The harness compares any
candidate against `baseline_dice` (the predecessor dice-based generator).

## How to change things safely

### Adjusting V/B/O quotas

1. Edit `SIZE_CONFIG[<size>].V` and `B` in `engine/board.ts`. Note: `O`
   is computed as `size² − V − B`; don't set it directly.
2. Run `npm run calibrate-letters` to measure the impact at N=1000.
3. Look at the scorecard — every cell should stay green vs baseline.
4. If you want to validate a quota change more carefully, use
   `npm run tune-quotas` and inspect the search neighborhood.

### Adjusting pool weights

1. Edit `SIZE_CONFIG[<size>].vowel` / `.backbone` / `.other` in
   `engine/board.ts`. Numbers are relative — they're normalized to a CDF
   internally.
2. Run `npm run calibrate-letters`. Pay attention to:
   - Common-word count + length-6+ count (don't regress)
   - Coverage and concentration (don't tank diversity)
   - Suffix counts (don't drop -ed / -er / -ing pattern density)
   - SUBTLEX tier counts (especially easy + medium — keep attainable
     totals up)

### Trying a totally different model

The calibration harness is generic. Add a new candidate in
`scripts/calibrate-letters.ts` under `POOL_CANDIDATES`,
`PER_SIZE_CANDIDATES`, or `THREE_POOL_CANDIDATES`. Run the harness — the
report compares your candidate against baseline + the current H8.

## Trade-offs we accepted

- **Daily seed reproducibility is config-dependent.** If you change
  `SIZE_CONFIG`, historical seeds resolve to different boards on the new
  version. `daily_results.board` stores the played grid, so historical
  display/scoring are unaffected. Same trade-off applied to the dice.
- **No reject sampling.** We considered re-rolling boards that fail
  quality thresholds. Decided against it because (a) it's a hidden
  source of latency, (b) it's harder to reason about determinism with,
  and (c) we got to a "no regressions" model without it.
- **The "impossible" tier is huge** (~131k of enable1 words). Most
  English words are dictionary-only and not in SUBTLEX. We accepted this
  rather than tightening enable1 itself, because trimming enable1 would
  change scoring semantics and reject otherwise-valid plays.

## Known limitations / follow-ups

- A Pareto-frontier search across multiple metrics (instead of a
  threshold-constrained single-objective tuner) might find slightly
  better quota points. We hit local maxima fast; small gains likely
  available.
- The SUBTLEX tier cuts (Zipf ≥ 4 / 3 / 2 / <2) are somewhat arbitrary —
  picked by inspection, not by Brysbaert-recommended cutoffs. Could be
  tuned if a particular tier turns out to feel different in play.
- The current tooling deliberately doesn't tune *toward* SUBTLEX tier
  counts. We measure them. If you want to push variety harder, the
  obvious next step is a Zipf-weighted objective in `tune-quotas.ts`
  (e.g., score = `3 × easy + 2 × medium + 1 × hard`).

## File map for future reference

- `engine/board.ts` — production generator, `SIZE_CONFIG` lives here
- `scripts/calibrate-letters.ts` — main calibration harness (N=1000+)
- `scripts/tune-quotas.ts` — grid search for V/B/O per size
- `scripts/measure-vowels.ts` — analytical + empirical baseline stats
- `scripts/dump-engine-config.ts` — one-shot generator that emits
  `SIZE_CONFIG` from raw dice frequencies. Used during the cutover. The
  raw dice arrays only live in this script now.
- `scripts/preprocess-subtlex.ts` — converts raw SUBTLEX-US into the
  compact `scripts/data/subtlex.tsv` used by the harness.
- `scripts/data/mit10k.txt` — MIT 10k common-words list (committed)
- `scripts/data/subtlex.tsv` — preprocessed SUBTLEX-US, **gitignored**;
  regenerate via `scripts/preprocess-subtlex.ts` if you want the tier
  metric
- `scripts/data/README.md` — attribution + regeneration notes
